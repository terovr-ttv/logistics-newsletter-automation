                          BETWEEN THE LINES
        Kelly Anderson Group / Impact Solutions | June 2026

Spot truck rates jumped 31% year-over-year in May. After three years of a freight recession that grew more familiar than the weather, that single number says the cycle has turned -- and it's turning fast enough that carriers who haven't repriced contracts this quarter are already behind. This issue: where rates go from here, why the driver shortage just got reclassified as a structural problem, a quiet but meaningful win for brokers in Florida, and the border-state enforcement wave reshaping risk on the road.

THE BIG STORY

The over-the-road recession is finally in the rearview. The latest U.S. Bank Freight Payment Index, produced with DAT, shows spot rates up 31.29% year-over-year in May, with contract rates up 9% [DC Velocity]. On a per-mile basis, spot moved from $1.89 in March to $2.14 in May, while contract climbed to $2.18. What's notable is that the rate surge happened alongside softer shipment volumes -- spot volumes fell from 1.36 million in March to 1.11 million in May. That's a capacity story, not a demand story, and it's the kind of inflection point fleets have been waiting for.

The capacity picture is tightening on multiple fronts. The International Road Transport Union now calls the global truck driver shortage a structural threat, with roughly 3 million unfilled positions worldwide and Europe alone expecting to lose 660,000 drivers to retirement by 2030 [The Loadstar]. IRU's data shows 63% of European operators say they're already turning down loads because they can't crew the trucks. The U.S. numbers aren't quite as stark, but the demographics are similar and the bottom line is the same: capacity isn't coming back the way it did after past downturns.

What this means operationally: shippers who built budgets on 2024 rate assumptions are going to be unpleasantly surprised, and carriers who can hold capacity through Q3 will see the strongest pricing leverage they've had since 2022. DAT's Patrick Pretorius put it plainly -- "the figures holding steady point to a lasting shift, rather than a temporary anomaly" [DC Velocity]. Container port congestion is reinforcing the trend on the import side, with global container terminals running at a four-year high in waiting volumes and ocean spot rates climbing past $3,300 per TEU on Asia-North Europe lanes [The Loadstar].

The cycle has turned. The question for the back half of the year is whether your network is positioned to capture it.

QUICK TAKES

A win for brokers in the post-Montgomery era. C.H. Robinson was dismissed from the Florida lawsuit tied to driver Harjinder Singh's fatal U-turn, after the plaintiff confirmed the 3PL had no role brokering the load and had blocked the carrier in question since January 2024 [FreightWaves]. The dismissal means the highest-profile crash case of the year won't be a test of broker liability standards post-Montgomery v. Caribe -- but the vetting documentation Robinson produced is a useful template for any brokerage building its own defensibility file.

Border states crank up CV enforcement. A joint operation in Wichita County, Texas, ran 93 commercial vehicle inspections in a single sweep, issued 109 equipment violation citations, and impounded 16 trucks [FreightWaves]. Arizona troopers placed a driver out of service for operating without a CDL, medical card, or USDOT number. California's CHP is stepping up hours-of-service enforcement near El Centro after a rise in CV crashes. The common thread: equipment condition, driver qualification, and HOS compliance are getting fresh scrutiny -- and "we'll fix it next quarter" isn't the answer.

Final-mile expectations keep ratcheting tighter. AlixPartners' annual home delivery survey found consumers now expect free delivery in 2.7 days on average, down from 3.5-plus a year ago, and 52% say they'll boycott a retailer after just one or two botched deliveries [DC Velocity]. Yet 64% of retail executives say home delivery still isn't profitable. The result: 55% of retailers now use carriers outside the UPS/FedEx/USPS triangle, and reliability has overtaken price as the top criterion in carrier selection for the first time in the survey's history. For final-mile fleets, that's an opening.

LTL leaders double down on service. Saia rolled out REV (Rapid, Expanded, Visible) this month, automating its guaranteed 10 a.m. delivery option network-wide and launching real-time turn-by-turn tracking [FreightWaves]. Old Dominion, meanwhile, hit its 16th consecutive year as Mastio's top-ranked national LTL carrier for quality, with a 0.5% cargo claims ratio and 99% on-time. The competitive bar in LTL keeps rising on service, not price -- and that's good news for any carrier whose differentiation strategy starts with execution.

Presidential firing power gets stronger. In Trump v. Slaughter, the Supreme Court overturned the 1935 Humphrey's Executor precedent, giving the president broad authority to remove members of independent agencies including the STB [FreightWaves]. The practical takeaway for rail-served shippers and carriers watching the proposed Union Pacific-Norfolk Southern merger: STB decisions may move faster, but they'll also move more politically. Expect a more activist board.

WHAT TO WATCH

Tariff refunds hit earnings. After February's Supreme Court ruling struck down the IEEPA tariffs, Treasury has already processed roughly $22 billion in refunds, with an estimated $166 billion total in play [Transport Topics]. Few companies have booked the recovery yet, meaning Q2 and Q3 earnings could carry surprise tailwinds for shippers heavy in imported goods -- and potentially fund inventory pre-buys that show up as freight demand.

Humanoids step into the warehouse mainstream. Agility Robotics announced a SPAC merger that could raise up to $620 million to scale its Digit humanoid robot, which is already deployed at GXO, Schaeffler, and others [DC Velocity]. Pair that with the driver and warehouse labor outlook, and the economics of physical automation in distribution are about to get a serious public-market stress test.

Yellow's ghost still haunts labor planning. A federal court ruled that Yellow's 22,000 Teamsters get nothing under the WARN Act despite the abrupt 2023 shutdown [The Loadstar]. The decision narrows what employees can expect when carriers fail suddenly -- and quietly shifts more of that risk onto drivers and dockworkers evaluating where to work.

THE BOTTOM LINE

The freight market has turned, capacity is structurally tighter than the last cycle suggested it would be, and the operators winning new business are the ones competing on reliability rather than rate. Whether you run line-haul or final-mile, the back half of 2026 favors carriers who invested in their drivers, their equipment, and their service story while everyone else was waiting for the rebound. It's here.
