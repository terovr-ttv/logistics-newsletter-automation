                          BETWEEN THE LINES
        Kelly Anderson Group / Impact Solutions | July 2026

OPENING

"Rates have not kept up with that pace of inflation," Schneider's incoming CEO Jim Filter told Transport Topics this week -- and the market is finally catching up. Spot rates jumped 31% year-over-year in May [U.S. Bank], tender rejections punched through 17% heading into July 4, and the longest freight downturn in memory looks well and truly over. Inside this issue: what the rebound really means, the post-Montgomery broker liability landscape, a cargo-theft wave that just won't quit, and the global driver math getting harder.

THE BIG STORY

The freight market has turned -- and this time it looks structural, not seasonal.

U.S. Bank's latest Freight Payment Index shows spot rates climbing from $1.89 per mile in March to $2.14 in May, with contract rates following from $2.09 to $2.18 over the same stretch [DC Velocity]. Spot volumes actually fell during that window, which is the tell: rates are rising because capacity is leaving, not because demand is surging.

Where's the capacity going? A meaningful chunk is being pulled out by the federal crackdown on non-domiciled CDL holders and non-compliant carriers -- the same enforcement wave that turned last year's Florida Turnpike U-turn crash into a national story. Filter, who took Schneider's wheel July 1, put it plainly to Transport Topics: "The amount of capacity that entered the market wasn't playing by the same set of rules as everybody else." He estimates carrier costs are up roughly 25% since 2020 while rates lagged badly -- and he doesn't expect capacity to grow again until that gap closes.

FreightWaves' Craig Fuller framed the July 4 week as the freight market's Super Bowl, with routing guides expected to fail Wednesday through Thursday as driver availability tightens. Brokers with on-demand capacity, private-fleet access, and clean carrier rosters are the ones getting the phone calls.

The catch: shippers are getting squeezed from every direction at once. China-to-U.S. West Coast ocean spot rates have run from $1,800 in March to more than $6,100 today -- a 239% jump that has nothing to do with demand and everything to do with the top 10 ocean carriers controlling roughly 90% of global capacity [FreightWaves]. Warehouse rents are climbing. Fuel surcharges are back in play even as retail diesel has now fallen eight straight weeks to $4.67/gal [FreightWaves]. And U.S. Bank's Jeff Pape warns shippers who don't refresh their assumptions "may risk planning on outdated cost assumptions."

For carriers, the message is simpler: the pricing power is back, but only for operators who can prove they belong on the road.

QUICK TAKES

Post-Montgomery broker liability comes into focus
C.H. Robinson was dismissed from the Florida U-turn wrongful-death suit roughly two weeks after being named, after showing no connection to the load or carrier [FreightWaves]. The real precedent-setter -- Montgomery v. Caribe Transport II -- returns to the Seventh Circuit this month. Meanwhile, Descartes rolled out AuditLog inside MyCarrierPortal, capturing snapshot-based documentation of carrier vetting decisions specifically to meet the new "reasonable care" bar. Expect defensible documentation to move from nice-to-have to table stakes.

Cargo fraud keeps escalating
A California driver was arrested in Indiana with nearly $2.9 million in tungsten oxide powder allegedly obtained through fraudulent documents en route to Japan [FreightWaves]. Separately, a Chicago federal judge sentenced a Lithuanian national to five years for running a multi-year ring that used at least 11 aliases, fake carriers, and load-board impersonation to divert roughly 90 shipments worth $10.1 million [Transport Topics]. ATRI pegs total industry losses at $6.6 billion annually. Identity verification at booking is now a security control, not a paperwork step.

Global driver shortage, worse than it looks
IRU's 2025 survey puts unfilled truck driver positions at about 2.9 million across 18 markets -- roughly 11% of the workforce [DC Velocity]. Europe is short 502,000 drivers, and about 20% of European drivers and 24% of Australian drivers will retire within five years. IRU's punchline: pay hikes alone are hitting a "wage wall." Cab conditions, secure parking, predictable schedules, and time at home now move the needle more than wages.

Florida attacks the parking problem
FMCSA, FHWA, and FDOT broke ground on projects that will add more than 900 truck parking spaces along the I-4 corridor, backed by a $180 million INFRA grant [Transport Topics]. The Orlando-to-Tampa stretch currently has 90 public spots serving 18,000 daily trucks. Construction on the first three sites finishes within a year -- a rare piece of infrastructure news that will directly affect HOS compliance for anyone running the Southeast.

Consolidation continues at the dealer and carrier level
Rush Enterprises acquired five Peterbilt of Louisiana dealerships, a collision center, and a PacLease operation across Baton Rouge, Houma, Lafayette, Lake Charles, and New Orleans -- cementing Rush as North America's largest Peterbilt dealer group [Transport Topics]. On the freight side, PLS Logistics picked up international forwarder AGL Group, and C.H. Robinson bought DeSpir Logistics to build out secure transport for high-value freight [DC Velocity]. The dedicated and specialty niches are where the deal flow is.

FedEx Freight steps out on its own
The June 1 spin-off makes FedEx Freight the largest pure-play LTL carrier in North America, with 26,000 dock doors [DC Velocity]. CEO John Smith told SMC3 Connections attendees the company added 500 LTL-focused salespeople and nearly 900 IT staff to stand up independently. Meanwhile, FedEx's MD-11 fleet -- grounded after last year's UPS crash -- is returning to service ahead of peak, with four aircraft back in the air [Supply Chain Dive].

WHAT TO WATCH

EU de minimis just ended
As of July 1, parcels under €150 entering the EU face a flat €3 customs duty, mirroring the U.S. move on Chinese ecommerce last year [The Loadstar]. When Washington did this, forwarder volumes dropped hard before settling around 75% of prior levels. Expect a similar reset in cross-border ecommerce flows -- and watch for platforms to pivot promotions toward the UK while it finalizes its own approach.

CBP tightens the screws on imports
New forced-labor guidance from CBP effectively requires importers to document every supplier in every production tier or risk denial of entry [The Loadstar]. Separately, a July 24 interim final rule adds detailed data requirements on postal imports valued under $2,500 -- CBP estimates a $100 million-plus bump in annual duties. Compliance teams and brokers should be talking now.

Ocean rate whiplash
With Middle East shipping still not normalized and carriers actively managing capacity, expect continued volatility on trans-Pacific rates through peak. Del Monte already flagged a $40-45 million ocean-freight-driven cost hit [Supply Chain Dive]. If your customers ship internationally, their landed-cost math is moving weekly.

THE BOTTOM LINE

The through-line this month is discipline paying off. Rates are rising because the industry is finally shedding capacity that shouldn't have been there. Brokers and carriers who documented, vetted, and trained well are the ones being trusted -- and paid -- as the market resets. The rebound rewards the operators who stayed ready during the downturn. That's a good place to be.
